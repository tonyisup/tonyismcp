# Words Are Only the Surface — Phenomenological Drift

Semantic drift is already deeper than most people recognize. But it's not the deepest layer. There's something beneath it — what I'd call **phenomenological drift**.

Even when two people agree on a definition of a word, the word sits inside a completely different web of emotional history, bodily experience, memory, identity, and subjective intensity for each person. One person's "freedom" feels like open space and dignity. Another person's "freedom" feels like instability and exposure. Same word, different weight in the nervous system.

This is why conversations feel sterile even when both sides are being honest. Each person is defending against a different form of suffering, and neither can see it because they're arguing about the *word* instead of the *feeling* inside it.

The mis-connection is worse than the non-connection. It's the state where we think we've established understanding — based on assumptions about shared meaning — when in reality we haven't even found the right starting point.

When you say "I understand," check yourself. Can you predict how the other person would feel at the edges? If not, you only understood the label. Not the weight.
