# Understanding Should Collapse Disagreement

A common comfort in conflict: "We just see it differently." It's meant to be conciliatory. I think it's usually a cop-out.

My experience — not theory, but direct observation of my own conversations and the conversations I've watched — is that when two people "perfectly understand each other and still disagree," at least one of them is incorrect about being understood. Or worse: they believe they've been understood, when in reality they haven't.

This doesn't mean there are no irreducible value differences. It means most of the time, when people invoke "we just disagree," they're using it to stop looking. Paraphrase is not comprehension. Restating someone's argument in your own vocabulary is not the same as inhabiting the conceptual world that makes that position feel natural to them.

A better question than "what do you mean?" is "can you predict what the other person would say about edge cases?" If you can't — and most people can't — then you haven't understood yet. And neither of you knows it.

The deepest test of understanding isn't repetition. It's prediction — knowing how someone will judge cases you haven't discussed yet. Until you can do that, "understanding" is an assumption, not an achievement.
