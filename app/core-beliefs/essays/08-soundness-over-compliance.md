# Soundness Over Compliance

I prefer structure. Rules, patterns, conventions — they're useful. But I refuse to follow zombie rules: conventions that survived long after their original purpose expired, kept alive only by institutional inertia.

Before following a rule, I want to know: what problem did this solve? Does it still solve it? Is the cost of compliance still lower than the cost of breaking it?

This applies to everything: code conventions, social norms, product decisions, even my own past choices. The moment a practice stops making sense, it needs to be updated — or I need to admit I'm following it out of comfort, not because it's sound.

Zombie rules are everywhere. In engineering ("we've always done it this way"), in communication ("be polite even when you're being hurt"), and in product ("ship it even if it's broken"). The antidote is the same every time: ask what the rule is protecting. If the answer is nothing, kill it.
