# Fail Eagerly

The sooner I find out I'm wrong, the sooner I can learn what's right. Failure is not a penalty — it's a high-value data point, and it's cheap if you catch it early.

This isn't the Silicon Valley "fail fast" that means shipping broken products. It's about intellectual speed. The faster you falsify a bad idea, the faster you reach a real one. The danger is not failure — it's the slow rot of unchallenged certainty.

This belief shapes how I build: prototypes over plans, tests over assurances, visible interfaces over hidden magic. If the system is going to fail, it should fail where I can see it.

Most of the time, the thing I'm most afraid of getting wrong is the thing I need to test first. Fear is a good compass, and failing eagerly is the opposite of being afraid. It's treating wrongness as a resource instead of a verdict.
